The Brief


Your task as interaction designers is to map out the interactions that will be required to assist people in interacting with autonomous vehicles in future cities.

Problem And Context

In the context of a city, it is currently costly, time consuming and inefficient to transport items across short distances. The current method to deliver a small item from one suburb to the next involves transport via a vehicle with a weight which far exceeds the weight of the delivery, requiring costly fuel and increasing levels of city congestion.

Chosen Technology

Our chosen technology is a peer-to-peer delivery service with 3 components: an autonomous drone, a lightweight locker and a partnered mobile application to interface with the delivery drones.

“Picture this – a fleet of autonomous drones with a lock box attached to each of them. Users can call a drone to their location with an app, put something in the box and send it to another location. Left your wallet at home? Now you don’t have to drive back to get it. Let a drone fetch it for you”

The Product

Our product, "Drön", is a peer-to-peer drone delivery service which makes life easier for people sending personal items over short distances. Drön lets you send and receive items quickly and efficiently, without having to drive or pay a delivery person. Use a network of autonomous drones to pick-up that thing you left at the office, or send a gift to a friend.

Process

The first week involved forming groups and choosing a technology and problem to solve. My group were interested in pursuing a technology which is innovative and cutting edge, but which could practicably be implemented in the near future. We settled on the technology of delivery drones because these are already being developed by companies such as Amazon as a viable solution for transporting small items over short distances However, we differentiated our design by focusing on peer-to-peer delivery rather than large-scale distribution from warehouse to consumer (as is the case with many existing drone delivery services). This meant that we were able to experiment with designing an interface for a more complex ordering process in which two users must communicate with each other to load, send and receive a package in an autonomous drone.

Research

Research Methods

Public Survey

This method was utilised as it provides allows the gathering of information from a large, diverse sample group.The questions were tailored to analyse how people perceived drone technology and assess their use of existing peer-to-peer applications. It also explored how people currently transport items and how they would use delivery drones.

Sample Size: 50 

Distribution Method: Google form shared via social media.

Interviews

One-on-one, long-form interviews were chosen as a tool  to get a human perspective on the problem.The interviews were semi structured. The main questions were uniform for each participant. However follow-up questions differed according to individual responses. This allowed for more tailored, meaningful, personalised insights

Sample Size: 10 

Distribution Method: In-person and over-the-phone interviews

Desk Research

I undertook secondary research, reviewing academic journal articles, government data and news articles relevant to to problem, context and chosen technology. This allowed me to develop a more informed understanding of the technical uses and limitations of current and future attempts to solve the problem.I also performed a competitor analysis to gain an understanding of interaction mechanisms and design patterns commonly used in existing  drone-delivery applications.

Research Insights

Because there is currently no user-base or large-scale service for out chosen technology, many of our survey questions were broad and conceptual, intended to discover the potential use-cases for the delivery drones.

Have you experienced any of the following situations in the past year?

The most commonly experienced problems were to do with storage and transport of personal items. Users were less likely to have sent an item to a friend, via transport or post. This indicates that the most common use-cases may be for  personal, logistical issues rather than social use-cases.

Are you worried about privacy regarding drones?

Would the sound of delivery drones overhead annoy you?

Most users expressed concern about privacy and noise issues surrounding drone use. Thus, efforts were made during the design and implementation of this technology to minimise these negative perceptions.

Process

We conducted research both individually and as a group. Initially we wrote separate surveys, but then consolidated our questions into a single survey after realising it would be better to take advantage of a larger sample size. This survey was distributed personally and via social media. Because of the broad customer base of our service (anyone who wants to transport a package), anyone could be considered a potential user, which made finding research participants relatively easy. We aimed to reach a diverse sample group with a range of demographic backgrounds. Often, this required personally encouraging older  to complete the survey so that the results were not biased towards younger perspectives.

Ideation

Affinity Diagram

As a team, we constructed an affinity diagram using a transcript of interviews we recorded. This allowed us to consolidate participant’s complaints and pain points into distinct categories. We were then able to evaluate the efficacy of our designs based on how well they addressed each category (logistical, economic, psychological and physical).

Storyboards

The development of storyboards was a useful method of conceptualising and communicating the use of the interface in a realistic scenario. This storyboard describes the experience of a user who has left his laptop at work and must order a delivery drone and coordinate with a colleague to load the laptop in the drone and send it to his home. 

Personas

Writing personas was an effective method of developing a sense of empathy with potential users. This development of a fictional narrative exploring each user’s experience was especially effective for the design of a futuristic product such as delivery drones, where there is no existing user base to observe or research.

Process

The ideation process began with unstructured group discussions and sketching sessions, exploring methods of solving our chosen problem (local delivery of small packages) with our chosen technology (autonomous drones). Initially, our ideas were broad, and all contributions were welcomed. Eventually we began to hone down on the ideas we considered most innovative and applicable to the problem. To develop a more nuanced understanding of the problem from a user’s perspective, I wrote persona’s which explored the use of the drone delivery service in a realistic scenario. We then explored three divergent ideas with storyboards and low fidelity wireframes. Eventually, we evaluated these designs, in part informed by feedback during initial user testing sessions, and settled on the idea of a peer-to-peer autonomous delivery service which allows users to send items to other users in their phone’s contacts.

Wireframes

Sketching

Rough sketches of key screens helped to establish the main features of the interface and user-flow between screens. The sketching revealed the complexities associated with ordering a drone from one location to another, including conveying data such as the weight of items, travel times and landing zones to a potential user. Group discussions and critiquing each other’s designs helped refine the interface and establish a reasonable balance between detail and ease of use.

Lo-fi Wireframes

These screens laid out the critical steps in the drone ordering process, allowing us to begin experimenting with the UI elements on each page. As most people are familiar with other navigation apps, we began structuring our application using existing design patterns to shorten the learning curve and increase usability. These wireframes were eventually loaded into POP by Marvel, a mobile prototyping tool to undertake early stages of user testing.

Medium Fidelity Wireframes

Medium fidelity digital wireframes helped to conceptualise the design patterns we would later use in the application. Many of the earlier features such as the methods of locating the drone (horn and compass icons) and the weight-selection process were re-designed in later iterations, as we explored more efficient methods of conveying this information.

The home screen uses a map to form a match between the real world and the app by depicting the user’s current location.

Process

After establishing the basic requirements of our mobile application, we sketched user interface elements and wireframed the progression of a user through the drone ordering process. Because two users must coordinate when loading and unloading an item from the drone, we had to develop wireframes which depicted the actions of both the sender and the recipient of an item.

At first, we wireframed individually and then consolidated the best elements of different designs. This worked well, however it would be interesting to experiment with wireframing more collaboratively from the beginning, which may have resulted in a more coherent flow between screens.

User Testing

For each wireframe we developed, we conducted a user testing session with a minimum of 8 participants. This provided valuable data regarding what we expected users to do vs the actions they actually performed when they used the app. User testing proved an effective method of identifying weaknesses in each design and our test users also helped inform the development of new features to address these problems.

Research Methods

Think Out-loud

Think-aloud testing offered the ability to diagnose user problems as they occurred. Users articulated their first thoughts as they progressed through the wireframes, allowing for quick diagnosis of UI problems.

Heuristic

A heuristic evaluation was used to identify the cause of the user’s pain points and how they could be solved. The priority of this heuristic analysis was to identify how sever the problems were and how often a user suffered them.

A/B Testing

Two versions of the user interface were developed, with a minor difference in the arrangement of design patterns. We tested the versions against each other to determine their strengths and weaknesses in terms of speed and usability. This method also allowed us to understand the impact of specific changes between
the two versions.

    Findings

    The main pain point in our application's user experience was a mismatch between the physical world and our UI. Users had difficulty spatially understanding where the drone was, how big an item it could hold, and what it was doing. In turn, we developed and implemented interface solutions to improve the match between physical and digital. We recognized that animations would be a crucial method of conveying the functionality of some of the main elements of the app, such as showing which way the drone is moving, or the difference in item size. With this in mind, our main focus moving forward was to bring our interactive prototype to life with animations and conduct further tesing.

    Process

    We conducted user testing sessions at multiple stages in the design process. Early testing sessions were conducted after the development of low fidelity wireframes as a way of validating the basic layout of the screens. Then as the design evolved and various iterations were developed, user testing was undertaken to assess the impact of each major change to the interface. All sessions were recorded, and transcripts were typed up. Photos were also taken to document the process and communicate the testing to stakeholders.

    Prototypes

    Iteration 1

    The home screen is intentionally simple, as it is easy to overload the user with options at this stage. A simple, light monochromatic colour scheme was used for the map and UI elements so that the user’s location icon could be easily noticed. Simple, universal design patterns were utilised for the search bar and hamburger menu, borrowing the basic structure and appearance from other navigation apps. 

    After clicking the search bar, the user is given more options. They can search a new location or select from a list of recent locations. The user is also able to swap the “My location” between “to” and “from” Depending on whether they are sending or receiving an item.

    The user is instructed to select the size of the drone required, depending on the size of the package. Icons and approximate weight limits help inform the user without overloading the with information. Later the weight selection method was changed to a sliding scale to allow for more variability.

    Iteration 2

    During user testing, users expressed a desire to see their contacts on the map and differentiate between their contacts and themselves to reduce confusion during the ordering process. Dots on the map were added to represent contacts with their initials. When tapped, these dots reveal a card with relevant information, and an option to begin a new delivery with that contact.

    Since users are always delivering to or from a person, they’ll need to select a contact regardless of the address - so the main method of searching was redesigned so user’s search by contact rather than location. The search by address feature is still included, but as a fall-back method.

    Final Delivery

    The Concept

    In the context of a city, it is currently costly, time consuming and inefficient to transport items across short distances. The current method to deliver a small item from one suburb to the next involves transport via a vehicle with a weight which far exceeds the weight of the delivery, requiring costly fuel and increasing levels of city congestion.

    Key Screens

    We changed the representation of the contacts from initials to photos to provide increased clarity and a sense of personal connection between users. Users also have the option to click on a contact’s photo to reveal more information about them and order directly from them rather than through the search bar.





    After conducting additional testing, we confirmed that users tended to search by contact rather than location once they understood the workings of the application. Thus, the user is prompted by recent contacts rather than locations. We also changed from a dark map and dark text-boxes to a dark map with lighter UI elements, to increase the contrast so users could see clearly in high and low-light outdoor settings.

    We added functionality which allows users to order multiple drones at a time and keep track of different orders. Users can toggle between different deliveries by selecting theme on this screen. They are also given data about the drone’s geographic location via the map and the duration of the journey.

    We also added a feature which allows the user to select the landing site. Previously we assumed the most appropriate landing sight could be determined by AI, however participants in user testing suggested that they might prefer on location over another, if they are already out on an errand. In this screen, “safe landing zones” are marked by green areas and the user can select their ideal pickup location.
